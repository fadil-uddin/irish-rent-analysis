# Executive Summary: Irish Rental Affordability Analysis

## Objective

This project analyses Irish rental market data to understand how average rents have changed across Irish counties over time and what the patterns suggest about regional affordability pressure.

The analysis uses the RTB Average Monthly Rent Report dataset, focusing on county-level quarterly rents from 2007Q4 to 2025Q4.

## Dataset and Scope

The analysis focuses on:

- Locations: 26 Irish counties
- Period: 2007Q4 to 2025Q4
- Bedroom category: All bedrooms
- Property type: All property types
- Metric: Average monthly rent in EUR

The project uses county-level data only. Town-level and smaller local-area observations are excluded from this version to keep the analysis clear and comparable.

## Key Findings

1. Average county-level monthly rent increased from EUR 778 in 2007Q4 to EUR 1,336 in 2025Q4.

2. This represents an estimated county-average increase of EUR 558, or 71.8%, across the analysed period.

3. Dublin had the highest latest county average rent at EUR 2,165 per month in 2025Q4.

4. Donegal had the lowest latest county average rent at EUR 1,001 per month in 2025Q4.

5. Cavan showed the fastest rent growth since 2007Q4, with growth of 91.9%.

6. In 2025Q4, Dublin rents were approximately 66.2% higher than the non-Dublin county average.

## Interpretation

The analysis shows that rental pressure has increased substantially across Irish counties since 2007. Dublin remains the highest-rent market, but the fastest percentage growth is not limited to Dublin. This suggests that affordability pressure is geographically broad and should not be interpreted as only a Dublin-specific issue.

The Dublin premium remains significant, with average Dublin rents far above the non-Dublin county average. However, growth in counties such as Cavan, Longford, Galway, Leitrim, and Waterford suggests that regional rental stress has also intensified.

## Practical Implications

- Housing affordability analysis should consider both absolute rent levels and percentage growth.
- Dublin remains the most expensive county-level rental market in this dataset.
- Regional counties with rapid rent growth may require closer monitoring, even if their absolute rent levels remain below Dublin.
- A fuller affordability assessment would require income data, household composition, and local housing supply indicators.

## Limitations

- The analysis uses registered tenancy data.
- Some observations may be missing or suppressed due to insufficient data.
- The current version focuses on county-level trends only.
- The analysis does not include household income data.
- Rent values are not adjusted for inflation.
- The analysis is descriptive and does not claim causality.

## Conclusion

Irish average rents increased substantially between 2007Q4 and 2025Q4. Dublin remains the most expensive rental market, but strong growth across several non-Dublin counties suggests wider regional affordability pressure. The project provides a clear first-stage descriptive analysis and creates a foundation for further work using income, inflation, and housing supply data.
